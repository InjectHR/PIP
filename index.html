Toolkit Setup

Use the toolkit as a process and drafting aid.

This toolkit is designed for Australian HR team members supporting clients or managers through performance improvement processes. It separates practical process guidance from legal-risk checks and template wording.

This is not legal advice. Before live use, check the applicable award, enterprise agreement, contract, workplace policy, medical or protected attribute issues, internal delegations, and legal advice where termination is being considered.

01 Basics

Performance management should be specific, documented and genuinely aimed at improvement.

What underperformance means

Underperformance is when an employee is not fulfilling their role to the required standard. Common causes include unclear expectations, skill gaps, lack of training, low confidence, personal issues, interpersonal issues, workplace conflict, or insufficient feedback.

What a PIP is for

A PIP is a structured, time-limited plan that gives the employee clear expectations, support, review points and a genuine opportunity to improve. It should not be used as a pre-written path to dismissal.

What belongs in a PIP

Use a PIP for performance issues that can be measured against role requirements, KPIs, quality standards, timeliness, accuracy or output. Separate misconduct, serious conduct, bullying, harassment or refusal issues may need a disciplinary process.

What HR must protect

Fairness, evidence, consistency, support, employee response, reasonable adjustments, confidentiality, role-specific expectations, decision-maker independence and a clean process file.

02 Process Map

Move from evidence to expectations, then review, warning, extension, closure or termination.

  1. 1

    Identify the problem

    Write down the behaviour, outcome or task gap, when it occurred, why it matters, and what needs to change. Gather evidence such as work samples, CRM records, quality checks, customer feedback or KPI data.

  2. 2

    Assess objectively

    Check the role description, contract, award or agreement, policies, KPIs, training history, resources, prior feedback, and whether the expected standard was clearly communicated.

  3. 3

    Meet with the employee

    Hold a private meeting, explain the concerns with examples, invite the employee's response, ask about barriers, and document any personal, medical, workplace or adjustment matters raised.

  4. 4

    Create the PIP

    Set two to five measurable goals, support actions, employee actions, manager actions, review dates, evidence sources and consequences if improvement is not achieved or sustained.

  5. 5

    Review progress

    Meet at the scheduled intervals. Record what was achieved, what was not achieved, the evidence relied on, the employee's response, support delivered, and the next required outcomes.

  6. 6

    Decide next step

    Close the PIP if improvement is sustained, extend it if more monitoring is reasonable, issue a warning if performance remains below standard, or consider termination only after proper process and advice.

Timing guide: internal materials describe monthly reviews over around three months, while training material also notes that around four weeks may be reasonable in some circumstances. Set timing by the role, seriousness, measurable goals, available support, award/agreement or policy requirements, and legal-risk profile.

Performance Improvement Plan: Customer Service Representative

CompanyAcme Retail Pty Ltd
EmployeeSarah Lee
Job titleCustomer Service Representative
ManagerJames Morgan
Discussion date30 September 2025
Review dates14 October, 28 October and 11 November 2025

Specific areas requiring improvement

  • Failure to meet daily call handling targets.
  • Incomplete or inaccurate data entry in the CRM system.
  • Delays in responding to customer email queries.

Examples and indicators of performance

  • Over the past six weeks, Sarah has averaged 22 customer calls per day against a team target of 35.
  • Weekly call audits in August and September showed a 26% data accuracy rate in CRM entries against a 95% target.
  • 13 customer email responses were delayed by more than 48 hours in September.

Required outcomes

  • Meet or exceed the team average of 35 calls per day.
  • Achieve at least 90% accuracy in CRM data entry as verified by audits.
  • Respond to all customer emails within 24 hours of receipt.

Employee feedback

Sarah acknowledged the performance issues and cited difficulties adjusting to a new CRM interface introduced in July. She also reported occasional IT issues that affected access to customer emails.

Manager tactics

  • Use CRM job aids and request clarification on any data fields as needed.
  • Prioritise emails using Outlook flagging tools.
  • Schedule 30-minute blocks in the morning and afternoon to clear email backlogs.
  • Request technical support promptly when IT issues arise.

Training, coaching and support

  • Refresher CRM training with the systems team by 2 October.
  • Weekly one-on-one coaching sessions with James to review data entry accuracy and workload planning.

Other matters raised

Sarah advised that she has had childcare challenges two mornings per week. She is arranging alternative care from next week.

Reasonable adjustments

Sarah requested a later start time of 9:30 am on Mondays and Wednesdays for the next two weeks, which James approved.

Consequences

If satisfactory progress towards improving performance defined in this plan is not met, it may lead to disciplinary action, including potential termination of employment.

Client Email: Preparing to Start a PIP

Use this when telling a client or manager what to prepare before commencing a PIP.

Subject: Preparing to commence a Performance Improvement Plan

Hi [Client name],

Before commencing a Performance Improvement Plan for [employee name], I recommend we first make sure the performance concerns are specific, evidence-based and connected to the requirements of the role.

Please gather the following before the initial meeting:

1. The employee's position description, employment contract and any relevant policy, award or enterprise agreement provisions.
2. The performance standards or KPIs that apply to the role.
3. Objective examples of the performance concerns, including dates, records, work samples, client feedback, quality checks, CRM notes or KPI reports.
4. Any prior feedback, coaching, training, review notes or informal conversations about the same concerns.
5. Details of the support, training, resources or supervision already provided.
6. Any information about workload, health, personal circumstances, workplace issues or reasonable adjustments that may need to be considered.

At the first meeting, the manager should explain the concerns clearly, provide examples, invite [employee name] to respond, ask whether anything is affecting their performance, and discuss what support may assist them to improve. The PIP should then document the required outcomes, review dates, employee actions, manager actions, support to be provided and the consequences if performance does not improve and remain at the required standard.

The aim should be to give [employee name] a genuine and fair opportunity to improve. If termination may become a possible outcome later, it is especially important that the process is well documented and that [employee name] is given a fair opportunity to respond at each key stage.

Kind regards,

[Your name]
Client Email: Preparing for a PIP Review Meeting

Use this before a scheduled PIP review.

Subject: Preparing for the PIP review meeting

Hi [Client name],

Ahead of the PIP review meeting with [employee name], please prepare a short evidence pack showing progress against each expectation in the PIP.

For each performance requirement, please identify:

1. What outcome was required.
2. What was achieved during the review period.
3. What evidence supports that assessment.
4. What support or coaching the manager provided.
5. Whether [employee name] completed the agreed employee actions.
6. Whether the manager completed the agreed manager actions.
7. Any explanation, concern or adjustment request that [employee name] has raised.

At the review meeting, the manager should discuss both positive progress and any remaining gaps. If the required standard has not been met, [employee name] should be invited to respond before any warning, extension or further disciplinary step is decided.

Please send through the review material before the meeting if you would like me to check whether the wording is sufficiently objective and whether any risk issues need to be managed.

Kind regards,

[Your name]

07 Termination After PIP

Termination should be a final resort after the concern, support, warnings and response have been properly handled.

Before proposing termination

Termination letter structure

  1. Identify prior correspondence, PIP dates, warnings and review meetings.
  2. State the disciplinary meeting purpose and confirm the employee had an opportunity to respond.
  3. Set out each allegation or performance concern with findings and evidence.
  4. Summarise the employee's responses and how the company considered them.
  5. Explain why the response does not resolve the ongoing concern.
  6. Confirm the decision, notice period, final employment date and final pay arrangements.
  7. Address garden leave, return of property, confidentiality and contact details.

08 FAQs

Quick answers for common PIP questions.

Do we need three warnings before terminating for underperformance?

No fixed number applies in every case. Fair Work guidance notes there is no general rule requiring three warnings or even one warning, but employers should usually give the employee a chance to fix performance issues. The safer question is whether the employee understood the concern, had a fair opportunity to improve, received appropriate support, and was warned of potential consequences.

Can we terminate as soon as the PIP ends?

Only if the evidence and process support it. Check whether the performance standard was clear, support was provided, review meetings occurred, the employee was heard, warnings were given where appropriate, and termination was put to the employee before the decision was finalised.

What if performance improves for one week?

Recognise the progress, but do not automatically close the PIP. The internal review example makes a useful distinction between short-term improvement and sustained improvement. If consistency is required, document the improvement and keep monitoring through the agreed review period.

Can behavioural issues go into a PIP?

Use care. Training material suggests PIPs are mainly for performance rather than disciplinary issues. Some minor or connected behaviours may be addressed through measurable expectations, but misconduct, serious misconduct, bullying, harassment or refusal issues may require a separate disciplinary process.

What should be in the PIP file?

Keep the PIP, meeting invitations, meeting notes, evidence reviewed, employee responses, support provided, manager actions, review outcomes, warnings, adjustment considerations, correspondence, decision-maker notes, final pay and notice checks, and any advice obtained.
